Responsive Web App | Client Prototype | 1 Month
REDACTED [product name] is a responsive web application which allows participants who are eligible for retirement the ability to process their pension election online. This replaces an arduous manual process where retirees needed to send forms back and forth in the mail over the course of 4-6 weeks.

The Business

REDACTED [company name], the pension department of a major international HR consulting firm, is a leader in administering and managing pension plans for their client companies. They provide critical retirement services for their clients and their clients' employees as they navigate an often difficult and confusion retirement landscape.


A Problem to Solve

REDACTED [company name] came to us with a very specific problem. Their current portal for pension employees did not provide the ability to submit relevant information and make election decisions to kick off their retirement. Employees had to mail paper forms back and forth to make their pension elections and could often take months to complete. Additionally, their competitors were using this as a way to win business.

Negative industry reputation for trailing with technology and being a “spreadsheet solution” by current manual processes


A Peek at the Solution

Below are some of the main features I designed for this client prototype, which ultimately won the business, based on the research and workshopping process.


01

To Do List Dashboard

A simple card based to do list style dashboard allows participants to complete the sections in a soft order and clearly follow their progress along the way.

02

Election Forms

When drilling into each section, full page overtake forms provide a focused experience for each major step in order to knock out one critical section at a time.

03

Submission Summary

After participants finalize and submit all sections, they can revisit the site to see their pension status and review their election decisions.


How did we get there?
The Process
It Starts with a Workshop

The best way to uncover the problem and establish a shared understand of the product vision, MVP, and roadmap is to get everyone in the same room for a day long workshop. This is a proven method – but only if everyone shows up both physically and mentally. There's invariably one person on the client side who always asks the question:




Question: “Do I really need to be there in person?”




Answer: “Yes, you do.”




At Devbridge Group ⇗, we were flexible on many things with our clients (especially, during the sales process); however, one of the best hard rules we established was in-person workshops. To really understand the problem, the product, the vision, the users, the tech, and establish a clear roadmap, you need the most important people physically in a room together.

Workshoping in person



What We Learned

After a full day working session, a demo of their current low functionality website, and informal interviews with recent retirees from our own network, we learned a lot about the pension election process.

Basically, it sucks.

It's often a complicated and confusing process for one of the most important financial decisions of your life. Here's some of our specific findings:

  • Physical forms sent back and forth take a long time and cause stress while waiting
  • Estimated calculations for monthly annuity payments are often not exactly correct and need to be changed, which is confusing and frustrating
  • Our client uses a literal spreadsheet (yes, an actual Excel spreadsheet) for their back-end technology and while it's accurate, it has given them a negative industry reputation
  • Many participants prefer the "white glove" personal service and may not enjoy a mandatory online experience, especially one with no direct human contact
  • The ability to process pensions online appears on many business's (but not employees) must-haves when signing up with a new pension provider
  • Oftentimes, participants children or grandchildren are doing the online work for them on iPhones, tablets, XBOX, etc. – especially for older folks who aren't used to modern web technology

Old School Manual Forms
What I Did

I knew the best way to communicate our understanding of the problem, was to build a simple clickable prototype to demo during our pitch meeting.

In light of our learnings above, I focused the prototype on the following objectives:

  1. Simplest path to Retire Online
    • Guided navigation, distinct view and edit modes, and clear progress indicators all help to make an often confusing experience, much simpler
    • Help is always available and it's clear what your options are the entire way
  2. Intuitive & Accessible
    • Since the typical retirement age is late 60s and oftentimes pensions are provided to companies whose employees often don't use computers regularly, this needed to be as basic as possible
    • Simple screens, large font sizes, and clear visual affordances will make this product easy to use for everyone
  3. Responsive Design
    • Card based layouts and full page overtake forms allows for flexibility on many different device types and sizes


How I Did It

Now that I knew my objectives and agreed with my product manager on the goals of this engagement, it was time to start sketching out some ideas.

Some Early Sketches

Early on when discussing the process and users, I was inspired by Intuit's financial products like Mint and TurboTax. The ability to guide users through a checklist of steps which ultimately ends in submitting a package of information applies just as much to tax filing as it does to pension processing.

After I started to become comfortable with the feel of the designs, I made a list of the features in the MVP I wanted to capture in this Retire Online experience:

  • Provide basic information about myself, my spouse, and any other relevant financial data
  • Understand my election options and make a decision
  • Save my progress
  • Ask for help if I need it
  • Review my submission
I was confident that if I could incorporate these features into a simple linear prototype, we had a good shot at winning the engagement.



How did it turn out?
The Prototype

Below you'll find a carousel of screens which I successfully presented via a clickable InVision prototype in our pitch meeting. Step through the slideshow below to check out entire pension election workflow.


Complete Prototype

This welcome screen is the first screen participants will see, so it's informative and sensitive - reminding users of their help options during the process was important.

Prior to diving into the dashboard, asking for their prospective retirement date brings personalization as well as verifies eligibility.

This is the main to do list dashboard. The card based layout with indicators on what can be completed now, allows for flexible screen sizes and provides a natural suggested workflow that isn't too restrictive. Prior to final submission, users will be taken to this as their homescreen upon returning.

Drilling into each section from the dashboard brings users to a full screen edit mode. This provides a focused experience, especially for retirees not comfortable with modern web applications, as they navigate the sections.

As participants complete the sections, they will see validation indicators along the way for which sections are completed and potentially, which sections need updated or additional information.

Time to make the main pension election decision.

After the necessary information and decisions are provided by the participant, they must sign some documents. The embedded document center allows for e-sign and physical uploads. Users will be able to review these documents after they submit, as well.

Once the dashboard is in this current all green state, the final Review and Submit step is unlocked and available.

After submitting their pension information and election decisions, participants can review their summary and processing status. This ends the online portion of the election process. Now, when users log in they will be taken to this read only summary page.




What did I learn from this project?
Reflections
Key Learnings
  1. Remember, you're always after the true business process and goal for the task. The software should simply enable its users to complete those tasks, not define how they're done
  2. Focus on problem => solution style thinking rather than interfaces in working sessions
  3. Don't make assumptions about users without research. Initially, I heard Pension Web App and immediately thought old desktop PCs, but later learned that wasn't completely true – participants children and grandchildren were doing this on behalf of the primary user from their own modern devices as well

All this to say, get the right people in the room, and the product vision will be clear.
